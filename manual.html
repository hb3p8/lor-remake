1 · The Idea of the Game

Marches of Aldermere is a kingdom of one castle on a wild, half-mapped frontier. You are its sovereign — but a sovereign of a peculiar kind. You build, you tax, you set the realm's priorities. What you cannot do is march a single hero anywhere by hand.

Your heroes are free agents. They are hired into your guilds, but once abroad they wander, fight, explore, loot, get drunk on their winnings, and die on their own schedules. You influence them the way a crown influences adventurers: with money. Post a bounty on a wolf den and the greedy will gather. Build a blacksmith and they will gear up. Build a market and they will buy healing draughts. Everything you want done in the wilderness, you must make worth their while.

Meanwhile the realm itself must not starve. Your fields feed your people; your people pay your taxes; your taxes raise your walls and your buildings; your walls and heroes hold off the raids that come, season after season, for your throne. Lose your population to famine or a breached gate and the game is over.

In one breath

You don't command heroes — you bait them. You don't farm — you site fields by water and let them grow. You don't fight the siege — you pay the guards and lure the heroes home in time.

2 · The Screen & Controls

The whole realm is rendered as a grid of ASCII glyphs — your overworld map — framed by a top status bar and a bottom command tray.

The top bar

Shows your vital ledgers: Food, Coin, Population (current / housing cap), the current season and turn count, and the prompt to advance time. Tapping the top bar's advance prompt resolves the next big turn.

The map

Only tiles your heroes (and your castle's own sight) have revealed are drawn; the rest is dark. Glyphs show terrain, buildings, your heroes, wandering beasts, raiders, villages and bounty markers. See the Glyph Glossary for the full alphabet.

Giving orders

You act by tapping a tile or a creature, which opens the context actions for that spot:

  • Tap your castle — open the build / upgrade / hire menu.
  • Tap a monster or a den — post or raise a kill / hunt / lair bounty on it.
  • Tap unexplored ground — post an explore bounty to lure scouts there.
  • Tap a hero — inspect its guild, level, HP, purse and gear.
  • Tap eligible ground near the castle — found a village.
Note

You never select-and-move a hero. The only way to draw one somewhere is to make that place pay.

3 · Turns & the Seasons

Time passes in big turns. Each big turn is the realm's full accounting period: the economy is tallied (food grown and eaten, taxes collected, population adjusted), then the world acts. Internally each big turn plays out as four sub-turns of movement, so heroes and monsters take several steps across the map per turn — but you decide and pay between big turns, not between steps.

The seasons

The realm cycles between summer and winter. Each season lasts a deterministic 10–20 big turns (per the world's seed), and the change is announced ahead of the frost so you can prepare. The season is the single biggest swing in the game:

Winter is the test

Fields produce nothing in winter. The realm eats through stored food and whatever the fisher and hunter still bring in. A kingdom that did not stockpile — or did not build winter-proof food sources — will watch its population starve. Plan every summer with the coming winter in mind.

4 · The Castle & Your People

Your castle is the heart of the realm: it is your throne, your granary, your hero-hiring hall, and the only thing that absolutely must not fall. It also defines your housing cap — the maximum population the realm can hold — and the number of guards that defend it.

Upgrading the keep

The castle climbs through four tiers. Each upgrade raises your housing cap and grants another standing guard, and is paid in food + coin:

Guards = castle tier + 1. Upgrade costs are paid from your stores after the turn's growth and build queue resolve, so a city won't starve itself to upgrade.

Population growth

People arrive when the realm is prosperous. When you are running a food surplus and have at least 18 stored food and housing to spare, growth accrues: roughly every 2 big turns of surplus adds +1 population, up to your housing cap. More people means more taxes — but also more mouths.

5 · Food, Fields & Winter

Food is scarce by design. The castle does not conjure food from nothing — it is fed by the farm fields around it and by dedicated food buildings.

Fields around the keep

When the realm is founded, the land gets only 4–6 single farm tiles, and they are placed where they can be: near water. Each farm field within 6 tiles of the castle feeds it directly, worth 0.8 food per big turn in summer — and zero in winter.

The food equation each turn

The Granary doesn't grow food — it makes your people eat less, cutting consumption to ×0.7. It is one of the cheapest and most reliable ways to survive winter.

The breadbasket plan

Your castle alone supports a town of perhaps 6–8 souls. To grow past that you must reach beyond the keep: build the fisher and hunter for winter-proof food, found villages by water for their fields and caravans, and place hunt bounties on fat beasts to top up the stores before the frost.

6 · Coin & Taxes

Coin builds everything that isn't grown. Your steady income is tax: 1.6 coin per population per big turn. A bigger, better-fed city is therefore a richer one — population is the engine of both your survival and your treasury.

Other ways coin flows

  • Shop taxes — when heroes spend their loot at your market and blacksmith, the crown skims a cut (see §9).
  • Guard kills — your salaried guards trickle a little coin (3 each) to the treasury for the monsters they fell.
  • Village caravans — villages cart in coin as well as food.

What coin pays for

  • Castle upgrades (coin portion) and all building construction.
  • Hiring heroes into your guilds.
  • Bounties — the prize money that lures heroes into the wild.
  • Founding villages.

8 · Heroes & the Guilds

Heroes are your only agents in the wilderness — and the least obedient. Build a guild hall, pay the hiring fee, and a hero appears at your castle to seek its fortune. There are four kinds, each from its own guild, each with its own appetite for risk.

R  Ranger

The scout and beastmaster. Long sight (vision 6) and the fastest legs of the guilds. Light armour, a quick bow (1d6), nimble (DEX-based). Earns extra XP for exploring new ground, and alone can tame wild beasts to fight at its side (§14).

Hire 80 coin · vision 6 · light · fights for less XP than it scouts.

r  Rogue

The opportunist. Cheapest to hire and surprisingly hard to kill once it learns to vanish. Picks fights it can win, extorts coin from your villages, and from the start can turn invisible to slip away from anything stronger (§15). Pays less shop-tax — it knows people.

Hire 35 coin · vision 4 · light · stealth from level 1.

F  Fighter

The front line. Heavy armour (AC 13), a real weapon (1d8), the deepest HP pool, and the full fight-XP rate. Slow and short-sighted, but the hero you want standing in the gate when raiders come. The backbone of any defensive plan.

Hire 150 coin · vision 3 · heavy · STR-based.

M  Monster Hunter

The elite slayer. The hardest-hitting blade in the realm (1d10), heavy armour, good sight, and built to walk into a lair and walk back out. Expensive to hire and gated behind the priciest hall — but the answer to trolls, crypts and the worst the frontier offers.

Hire 190 coin · vision 5 · heavy · STR-based.

What heroes do on their own

Left to themselves, heroes patrol the explored land, hunt nearby threats they think they can beat, chase posted bounties, return to the castle to rest when wounded, and visit your shops to spend their loot. A hero below half HP will break off and head home to heal. They will not suicidally charge things far above their level if they can help it — but heroes are greedy and brave, and they do die.

9 · The Hero Gold-Loop & Shops

This is the engine that turns your heroes into a self-improving — and self-funding — adventuring economy.

  1. Heroes loot. Killing monsters and clearing ruins fills a hero's personal purse (e.g. a wolf yields 7 coin, a bandit 13, a bear 22, a cleared lair 160 split among the assaulters, a fresh ruin 35).
  2. Heroes spend. When they pass the castle they buy from whatever you've built:
    • Blacksmith — gear. Each tier (up to 5) costs 40 × (tier+1) coin (40 / 80 / 120 / 160 / 200) and grants +1 to-hit per tier, +1 damage and +1 AC every two tiers, and +3 max HP per tier. Heroes buy entry-tier gear early and cheaply, so they're armed before they're rich.
    • Market — potions. A flask costs 5 coin, heals a flat 10 HP, and a hero carries up to 3. Cheap on purpose: heroes sip them repeatedly through a fight rather than hoarding for one big heal.
  3. The crown skims. Every purchase is taxed back to your treasury — a base 35% cut, +20% more if you've built the Market. (Rogues, being well-connected, pay only 70% of that rate.)
Why this matters to you

A blacksmith and a market don't just keep your heroes alive and strong — they convert monster loot you'd never see into crown income. Building them is both a war measure and a tax policy.

10 · Combat & Levelling

Fights are resolved in the old dice tradition. When two creatures stand adjacent, the attacker rolls a d20, adds its attack bonus, and compares against the defender's armour class. A hit rolls the damage die plus modifiers; a natural 20 lands hard. Tight packs of attackers get extra riposte swings, so being swarmed is genuinely dangerous.

Healing mid-fight

A hero that drops below 70% HP with a live enemy adjacent will quaff a potion on the spot (a purple flash marks it), healing 10 HP and sipping again as needed while flasks last. This is the difference between a hero who limps home and one who dies in the grass.

Earning levels

Heroes gain XP for the damage they deal (2 XP per point) and a bonus per kill (10 for a boar, 14 a wolf, 18 a bandit, 32 a bear, 55 a troll, plus pools for cleared lairs). Rangers and rogues additionally earn XP for revealing new ground. Each level needs 100 × level XP; heroes climb to a cap of level 6, getting tougher, deadlier and more reckless with every step. A yellow flash marks a hero coming into coin or a new level.

The frontier is populated by three classes of danger: harmless-until-provoked beasts, opportunistic humanoids, and outright monsters. Clearing a lair yields a fat purse (160 coin, split among the heroes who stormed it) and a shared XP pool. These are the great prizes you post lair bounties for.

12 · Raids, Guards & the Siege

The evil kinds — bandits, goblins, skeletons — do not merely lurk. On a recurring cadence the wilderness sends a raid wave toward your castle. Waves grow larger as the game goes on (up to 4 raiders, more against a fat undefended city), and winter brings them sooner and harder.

Your guards

The castle keeps standing guards — castle tier + 1 of them (2 at start, up to 5 at a Stone Keep). Guards (G) patrol within a defence radius of 11 tiles, intercepting and chasing hostiles that come near. A fallen guard is replaced after 3 big turns. Guards are solid but deliberately not invincible — a guards-only kingdom can be overwhelmed.

The siege

When raiders actually reach the castle's doorstep, a siege resolves. Defenders are your surviving guards plus any heroes within 6 tiles of the keep. The raiders that outnumber your defenders breach the walls — and each breaching raider kills one population. Lose all your people and the realm falls.

The hardest lesson

Heroes only help hold the gate if they are near it when the siege lands — and heroes love to be off chasing bounties. A classic defeat is a wave arriving while every hero is across the map getting rich. Keep a fighter home, or post bounties near the castle as the season turns, so your blades drift back before the raiders do.

13 · Bounties — Your One Lever

Bounties are how you command without commanding. You can't order a hero to a spot — but you can hang gold on it, and the greedy will come. Tap a target to post a bounty; tap again to raise it (+25 coin per tap, capped at 150) or cancel it. A richer bounty draws more — and braver — heroes.

Bounties are your whole hand of cards

Need the map opened? Explore bounty. Need food before frost? Hunt bounty on a bear. Crypt spawning raiders onto your walls? Lair bounty — and raise it until a hunter bites. Every priority you have is expressed as a price.

14 · Beast Taming

A ranger alone can befriend the wild. Bring a ranger near a beast it's high enough level to tame and it may, instead of killing it, tame it into a loyal companion that fights at its master's side.

BeastMin ranger level
Boar2
Wolf3
Bear5

The base chance is 35% at exactly the minimum level, rising +15% per ranger level above it. A tamed beast gets a much deeper HP pool, slowly regenerates, ranges out to engage threats near its master (and catches back up if it falls too far behind), and even levels up itself (to a cap of 3) as it fights. A high-level ranger with a tamed bear is one of the strongest field units in the game — and costs you nothing in coin.

15 · The Rogue's Trade

Rogues fight poorly head-on but earn and survive in ways no other guild can. They are the realm's chancers.

Extortion

A rogue standing beside one of your own villages can shake it down — pocketing 14 coin and earning 6 XP, then cooling off for 3 big turns before it can do so again. This lets a rogue gear up and level even before it's won a single real fight — but the coin comes out of your village's prosperity, so it's a hero getting strong on the realm's back.

Stealth

From level 1, a rogue that is outmatched can vanish for 4–6 sub-turns (3 big-turn cooldown). While stealthed it's drawn dim and is ignored by enemies — skipped in combat, stalking and targeting — giving it a real chance to break off and escape a fight it would lose. It also earns a little XP for exploring (30% of a ranger's rate), and pays reduced shop-tax. Rogues are still the most likely guild to die young, but stealth keeps the boldest of them alive long enough to matter.

16 · Villages & Caravans

Villages are how you push the realm's reach — and its breadbasket — beyond the castle's own small fields. Tap eligible ground to found one (paid mostly in coin), and it becomes a self-tending settlement that farms, sends caravans home, and lights up the map around it.

Founding rules

  • Must be at least 8 tiles from the castle and 6 tiles from any other village; up to 4 villages total.
  • Costs scale progressively — each village you already hold makes the next one dearer (×(1 + 0.5 × held)), so sprawl is throttled like castle upgrades.
  • The founding screen shows the full ledger: distance, path cost, and the food/coin a site will actually yield, before you commit.

Village fields & the water rule

A village's food comes from the farm fields it can raise nearby (up to 6, each worth 1.7 food) — but fields can only be sited within ~8 tiles of water. A village far from any river is a poor farm. Yield is further reduced by proximity: villages crowded near the castle or each other suffer a penalty (down to a floor of 40%), rewarding you for spreading your settlements across good, well-watered ground.

Caravans

Roughly every 6 big turns a village that has built up enough surplus dispatches a caravan (a noncombatant cart, role cart) carrying food and coin to the castle. Caravans are vulnerable on the road — they can be raided and lost.

Raids & decay

Villages aren't safe. Raiders may divert to attack a village (each raid does 6 damage to its 24 HP); a village has a small defence radius of its own. A village starved of attention or cut off can decay, and when it falls its improved tiles revert to their original wild terrain. Villages also serve as vision sources, keeping a 6-tile patch of the map lit even with no hero present.

Founding well

The best village sits near water, away from the castle and other villages, and not too deep in raider country. One good breadbasket on a river can carry your whole realm through winter; a dry, crowded village barely pays for itself.

17 · A Steward's Counsel

Open the map first

Hire a cheap ranger or rogue early and post explore bounties outward. You can't site villages, hunt beasts or pre-empt lairs you haven't seen.

Winter-proof your food

Fields freeze. Build the fisher and hunter (they work in winter), get a granary to eat less, and bank a bear hunt or two before the frost.

Population is the engine

More people = more tax = more everything. Keep a food surplus flowing so the town grows to its housing cap — then upgrade the keep to raise the cap.

Don't trust the guards alone

Guards thin out under big waves. Keep at least one fighter home, or bait your heroes back with castle-side bounties as a season turns.

Build the shops

Blacksmith + market keep heroes alive and strong and tax their loot back to you. They pay for themselves twice over.

Raise bounties that aren't biting

A bounty no hero takes is too cheap for the risk. Tap to raise it — gold is the only voice your heroes hear.

Kill the crypt

An undead crypt spawns skeletons that march on you. Don't tolerate one within striking distance — post a lair bounty and pay a hunter to end it.

Spread your villages

Site them on rivers, far apart, out of the worst raider country. A clustered village earns the proximity penalty and barely feeds itself.
