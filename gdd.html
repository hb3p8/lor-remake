Game Design Document

Marches of Aldermere

A turn-based frontier lordship game: grow a castle's population, raise the buildings that put settlers to work, hire wandering heroes with gold, and watch autonomous folk go out into a dangerous procedural wilderness to farm, hunt, cut timber, guard the marches, and delve old ruins.

Version 0.3 Majesty-aligned reset Genre: strategy / simulation / light RPG Mode: single player

1. Core Concept

The player is a minor medieval lord holding the outer marches. The crown wants taxes, safer roads, and expansion; the frontier offers timber, game, ore, ruins, and old dangers. The game pairs a procedural world map with a castle / city management view.

The player never moves a unit tile by tile. Instead the player grows the city's population, builds structures that define job slots, assigns settlers to those jobs, and hires self-directed heroes with gold. Every worker and hero is a real, autonomous unit on the map that pursues its profession on its own — in spirit a blend of Majesty (autonomous agents, gold-lured heroes) and a simplified Heroes of Might & Magic town screen.

Design intent: the player rules a frontier system. You decide who exists and what they are paid to do; they decide how to do it. A well-run city has fed settlers in the right jobs, sturdy guards holding the work radius, and a hero or two clearing what ordinary folk cannot.

2. Current Prototype Foundation

The single-file prototype already establishes the board and the autonomous-agent simulation the management layer will sit on top of.

World

100 x 60 procedural overworld: terrain, elevation, hydrology (priority-flood rivers and lakes), slope-aware A* roads with shared trunks, settlements with grown farm and town tiles, ruins, fog of war.

Autonomous Agents

Units hold one explicit goal at a time (explore, hunt, farm, patrol, delve, resupply, rest, flee), plan a danger-aware A* path, and move on a per-turn step budget with animated resolution.

Threat & Combat

~40 spawned hostiles (boar, wolf, bear, bandit, goblin) with territory, sleep/wake culling, stalking, and a full d20 combat model with packs, flanking, fight-or-flight, and a detailed battle log.

♜ = player castle ⌂ = towns / villages ▦ = farms ♣ = forest ▲ = mountain ‼ = ruins ~ = river / lake = = road / bridge * = marsh

Direction shift (v0.2): the four fixed starter characters (scout, hunter, guard, farmer) are being folded into a population + job-slot model. Ordinary professions become settlers assigned to building slots; the scout is removed for now and returns later as the first hireable hero.

3. Design Pillars

Build, Assign, Hire

The player's three levers: raise buildings (which create job slots and unlock heroes), assign population to those jobs, and spend gold on autonomous heroes. No direct unit orders.

Frontier Uncertainty

The map begins hidden by fog. Terrain, ruins, hostiles, and resource sites become known through scouting and lived travel.

Economy With Consequences

Food governs population; coin and timber gate buildings and hires; guards hold the work radius. Mismanagement shows up as a stalled, hungry, raided city.

Low Magic, High Folklore

Magic is rare, costly, and local. The world reads medieval first, mythical second.

4. Core Game Loop

The game runs on big turns. At each big turn the player makes all decisions, then commits; the world then resolves over a fixed number of sub-turns (currently 4) before control returns. There is no auto-advance — the player chooses when to take the next big turn.

  1. Review: the digest of the last big turn (what heroes did, threats, kills, losses, income), explored terrain, population, food/coin balance.
  2. Decide & commit (big-turn boundary only): start a building, upgrade the castle, found a village, hire from a guild. (Reward bounties come later.)
  3. Resolve sub-turns: the committed plan plays out over 4 sub-turns — heroes, guards, and hostiles move, fight, flee, and explore. The economy ticks once per big turn (food, population, taxes).
  4. React: a digest summarizes the sub-turns; detailed blow-by-blow lives in each hero/hostile detail screen.

A sub-turn is one unit's worth of movement + combat (the old "turn"). Bundling 4 into a committed big turn — rather than just widening step budgets — keeps combat interactions (multi-round melee, flee, intercept) intact while making the player's cadence strategic, not twitchy. One big turn represents roughly one week.

5. Population & Economy Rebuilding (Phase 1)

Following Majesty, the player no longer assigns labor. Workers are gone; population is an abstract number of townsfolk — not units on the map. The only embodied units are heroes, guards, hostiles, and (later) villages.

The food → population → tax loop (a flow)

  • Food is a flow. Each big turn, food production (a base castle "farms" income plus food buildings) is weighed against consumption (population × per-head, reduced by a granary). Surplus is stored; deficit drains the store.
  • Population grows on a food surplus up to the housing cap (set by castle tier), and consumes more as it grows — settling toward an equilibrium. A deficit halts growth (no starvation deaths for now).
  • Taxes turn population into coin each big turn. More people → more coin. (A market adds a flat bonus now; later its income scales with hero loot-selling.)
  • Heroes do not eat — they are coin-funded and self-sufficient (no abstract supplies; equipment/consumables come later).
  • Food grows faster in summer than winter, so the early game (mostly summer) is an economic ramp.

Resources are reduced to food and coin (timber/stone removed). Cosmetic fields auto-generate around the castle at map-init and do not affect the numbers.

Asymmetric gating: most buildings and tech cost only coin; the big structural moves — castle upgrade and founding a village — cost food. So coin buys breadth, food buys growth.

6. Buildings & Castle Rebuilding (Phase 1)

Buildings are bought from the abstract castle screen and are duration-based (pay coin, then construction takes a number of sub-turns; no population speed-up, food keeps flowing while building). Each provides a passive effect or unlocks hiring.

BuildingEffectNotes
GranaryLowers food consumption per headStretches a given food income further.
Fisher Hut+food; works in winterRequires a river/lake adjacent to the castle.
Hunter Hut+food; works in winterRequires woods adjacent. Passive (replaces the hunter worker).
Market+tax incomePricey but strong. For now a flat bonus; later scales with hero loot-selling.

Guild buildingsUnlock hero hiring (ranger / fighter / rogue / monster-hunter)See §7. Reuse the existing slot/hire system.

Castle tiers

The castle upgrades along a named ladder: none → wooden palisade → motte-and-bailey → stone keep. Each upgrade costs food, grants +1 guard, and unlocks higher housing limits and new buildings. The castle does not fight on its own — it relies on its guards (see §10).

7. Heroes & Guilds Later (Phase 4)

Heroes are the embodied agents the player invests in — hired for coin from guild buildings, not fed by the food economy, hired at level 1 / 0 XP (leveling comes later). Each guild biases the kingdom's autonomous behavior (the Majesty insight); all heroes act on innate guild priorities for now and will weigh reward bounties once those exist.

GuildBehaviorRole
RangersExplore actively even without rewards; not very strong. (Derives from the retired scout.)Information opening — reveal the map and its dangers.
FightersHelp defend the castle from hostiles, but unlike guards they roam the map and level up.Safety / offense — a mobile martial backbone.
RoguesAlmost free to hire, but each one drags taxes; weaker than fighters.Greed opening — cheap bodies at an economic cost.
Monster HuntersActively track and hunt monsters for explicit rewards; expensive, and strong even at low level.Specialist anti-monster force.

The retired Scout returns as the Ranger. Heroes rest and heal at the castle and at friendly villages.

7b. Villages Later (Phase 5)

There are no pre-placed villages; the player founds them one at a time (the map-gen still picks settlement sites and connecting roads — reused here: expanding claims the nearest site and lays a road to it). Founding costs food.

  • Villages are autonomous and pay extra taxes, but must be protected — they can be raided.
  • They are sources of visibility and let heroes rest and heal away from the castle.

8. Autonomous Behavior & Goal System Built

Every worker, hero, and hostile is driven by the same lightweight goal system, already implemented.

FieldPurpose
TypeThe intent: explore, hunt, farm, patrol/defend, delve, resupply, rest, flee, deliver.
TargetTile, resource site, hostile, or the castle.
PathA danger-aware A* route, recomputed only when stale or invalid.
CommitmentHolds a goal for a couple of turns to avoid thrashing, while still allowing urgent interrupts (wounded, outmatched, blocked).
UtilityWhy this goal was chosen; feeds report text.

Goal selection runs at decision points (no goal, target reached, blocked, wounded, danger exceeds confidence, stale), not every step. A per-turn danger field stamped by visible hostiles steers non-combat units around threats and feeds the fight-or-flight check; guards ignore it and instead seek hostiles to intercept, but never leave their defensive remit.

9. Headless Management AI — the Steward Built

The management layer (build, assign, hire) is the player's job — but two things demand an automatic stand-in for it:

  • The simulation harness runs headless with no player. Once population, job slots, and buildings exist, a run with nobody assigning settlers would just idle: no workers, no output, nothing to validate. The harness needs something to pull the levers so the economy loop can be measured.
  • Turns auto-advance even when the player is passive. A city that does nothing while the clock runs feels broken, so a sensible default is useful in the live game too.

The answer is a small, separate decision layer — the Steward — that operates the management levers with greedy, legible heuristics. It is deliberately distinct from the per-unit goal AI of §8: that AI decides how a worker does its job; the Steward decides which jobs exist and who fills them.

Principles

  • Deterministic. No wall-clock or unseeded randomness, matching the existing engine constraints, so harness runs stay reproducible.
  • Separate and optional. A distinct module the harness always enables, and which the live game can run as an "auto-steward" assist the player overrides at any time. It is scaffolding and a balancing tool, not the shipping player fantasy.
  • Legible priority order: don't starve → hold the line → grow housing → expand the economy → spend surplus.

Heuristics (first pass)

PriorityRule of thumb
1. FeedIf food is falling or below a buffer, assign idle settlers to farmer / hunter slots until the trend is positive.
2. DefendMaintain a guard floor (as population and barracks allow) so the work radius and farms stay held.
3. GrowWhen population sits at the housing cap with a food surplus, upgrade the castle (raises cap + work radius).
4. ExpandOnce fed and defended, put surplus settlers on woodcutters (later miners) and buy the building that unblocks the next bottleneck.
5. SplurgeHire a hero when gold comfortably exceeds a threshold and a guild exists.

The Steward runs each turn (or every few turns) before resolution. The harness enables it via a newGame option / an autoManage() step so it can drive a full game unattended and report the resulting economy.

Phasing rule: every management feature ships with the matching Steward heuristic in the same phase. If the harness can't operate a new lever, that lever isn't done.

10. Threats & Combat Built Rebalancing

Hostiles occupy map cells, move during resolution, and fight autonomous units. They have a type, territory (origin + radius), and sleep when far from any vision source for performance. Combat is automatic and uses a simplified d20 model:

  • Attack: d20 + attack bonus vs. the target's armor class; natural 20 crits (extra damage dice), natural 1 always misses.
  • Damage: per-unit damage dice subtract from an HP pool; fights play out over several turns via a zone-of-control melee lock rather than resolving instantly.
  • Advantage / disadvantage: favorable terrain, pack flanking (2+ attackers), and a fleeing target's exposed back.
  • Packs: multiple adjacent hostiles gang up (capped), making wolf and goblin packs genuinely dangerous.
  • Fight-or-flight: outmatched or wounded units break off and retreat to the castle; guards hold ground far more stubbornly.
  • Battle log: a per-swing record (rolls, advantage, damage, remaining HP, crits, kills) surfaces in the turn report.

Known balance work (in progress): a recent fix made high-cost terrain (mountains) passable again, which also freed bears to roam; observed lethality rose. Combat / terrain rebalancing toward sustainable losses is a tracked follow-up and a prerequisite for population mattering (guards drawn from a small population must not death-spiral).

13. Setting & Magic Tone

Medieval frontier fantasy with restrained magic: relics, old stones, curses, hedge charms, strange weather, and rare practitioners. Magic solves specific problems at a cost rather than replacing economy and logistics; most characters are mundane. Supernatural sites feel old and local, not cosmic.

14. Scope & Phasing

Direction reset (v0.3). The worker / labor-assignment model (old Phases 1-3) proved fiddly and added only visual noise, and the multi-resource economy felt redundant. We pivot closer to Majesty: abstract population, a food→tax flow, indirect hero ecology, and a committed big-turn / sub-turn loop. The phases below supersede the old ones (kept in git history). The Steward still drives every new lever headlessly so the harness keeps validating.

15. Decisions & Open Questions

Decided (v0.3 reset)

  • Drop workers / labor assignment and the timber/stone resources. Keep food and coin only. Population is an abstract number, not units on the map.
  • Food is a flow: production (base castle farms + food buildings, summer>winter) vs consumption (population, reduced by a granary). Surplus grows population up to the housing cap; deficit halts growth (no starvation deaths yet).
  • Population pays taxes (coin) each big turn. Heroes do not eat and use no abstract supplies.
  • Most buildings/tech cost coin; castle upgrades and founding villages cost food. Buildings are duration-based (coin then time; no population speed-up, food keeps flowing).
  • Cosmetic fields auto-generate at init; no pre-placed villages/roads (map-gen sites reused later for expansion).
  • Turn loop: committed big turn → 4 sub-turns, no auto-advance; economy ticks once per big turn. Digest reporting; detailed battle log only in detail screens.
  • Castle tiers none→palisade→motte→stone; each grants +1 garrison guard and unlocks housing/buildings. Castle doesn't fight; guards do, and respawn on a cooldown when killed.
  • Heroes hired for coin at level 1 from guild buildings (ranger/fighter/rogue/monster-hunter); act on innate guild priorities. Bounties deferred to backlog.
  • Winter changes terrain costs (all harder, mountains impassable, rivers ~ roads); stranding is accepted for now. Market = flat tax bonus for now; blacksmith inert for now.
  • Raid pressure comes from timed raid waves: evil warbands spawn at the frontier and march on the castle (ignoring territory/sleep), scaling with prosperity / wave count / winter. Guards patrol the keep when idle. Kept light until heroes can help defend; lairs/respawn are the durable long-term source (backlog).

Open

  • Exact food/tax/build numbers and the season length (10-20 turns) — to be tuned via the harness.
  • Whether sub-turn resolution should interrupt early on a major event (hero death, castle attack) or always run the fixed 4.
  • Village founding range/cost and how their taxes/defense scale.
  • Guard respawn cooldown length; how population loss from settlement attacks is paced.
