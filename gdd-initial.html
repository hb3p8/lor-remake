Initial Game Design Document

Marches of Aldermere

A turn-based frontier lordship game about building a small castle economy, hiring autonomous adventurers, revealing a dangerous wilderness, and folding distant roads, farms, ruins, villages, and native settlements into a fragile border realm.

Version 0.1 Based on ASCII overworld prototype Genre: strategy / simulation / light RPG Mode: single player

1. Core Concept

The player is a minor medieval lord appointed to the outer marches of a larger country. The crown expects taxes, resources, safer roads, and eventual expansion. The frontier offers timber, game, ore, old ruins, abandoned keeps, local trade, and ancient dangers.

The game combines a procedural world map with a castle management view. The player does not directly control every unit. Instead, the castle creates incentives, infrastructure, and employment. Hired characters explore, hunt, patrol, trade, and delve with semi-autonomous behavior, similar in spirit to Majesty, while strategic development resembles a simplified Heroes of Might and Magic town screen.

Design intent: the player should feel like a ruler shaping a frontier system, not a commander moving every pawn. Good decisions create roads, markets, safe routes, capable parties, and a stronger settlement. Bad decisions leave heroes underpaid, roads unsafe, farms raided, and the castle short of coin before winter.

2. Current Prototype Foundation

The existing prototype already establishes the strategic board language: a 100 x 60 procedural overworld with terrain, elevation, hydrology, roads, distant settlements, farms, ruins, travel cost, slope-aware A* routing, panning, rerolling, and tile inspection.

Terrain

Deep water, water, shore, river, bridge, plains, meadow, farmland, forest, deep wood, marsh, hills, mountains, peaks, roads, towns, castle, ruins.

World Structure

Non-player settlements are generated more sparsely on flat land near water, receive surrounding farms, and are connected by roads that prefer cheaper terrain and shared trunks. The player controls only the castle-settlement, which begins without farm fields.

Exploration Hooks

Ruins appear in dangerous terrain such as deep woods, mountains, and swamps. Roads, rivers, bridges, forests, and settlements already imply meaningful travel choices.

3. Design Pillars

Rule Through Systems

The player builds, funds, taxes, posts bounties, grants charters, stocks markets, and improves roads. Characters decide how to pursue opportunities inside those rules.

Frontier Uncertainty

The map begins hidden by fog of war. Terrain, ruins, hostiles, native settlements, and trade opportunities become known through scouting and lived travel.

Economy With Consequences

More economy than Majesty: food, timber, stone, iron, coin, labor, taxes, wages, market supply, upkeep, seasonal pressure, and road safety all matter.

Low Magic, High Folklore

Magic exists, but it is rare, costly, local, and unsettling. The world should feel medieval first, mythical second.

4. Core Game Loop

  1. Review the frontier: inspect explored terrain, threats, roads, resource sites, farms, settlements, and character reports.
  2. Manage the castle: choose buildings, taxes, production priorities, market stock, wages, bounties, edicts, and defensive preparation.
  3. Issue incentives: post exploration rewards, monster bounties, road patrol contracts, ruin-delving charters, trade route bonuses, or native diplomacy missions.
  4. Resolve the turn: autonomous characters travel, reveal fog, fight, flee, hunt, farm, trade, consume supplies, gain experience, and return with coin, goods, wounds, rumors, or losses.
  5. React: hostiles migrate, farms produce or suffer, villages trade or complain, roads become safer or more dangerous, and new opportunities emerge.

One strategic turn should represent roughly one week. A week is long enough for travel and economy to resolve without making each decision feel like a tiny tactical step.

6. Turn Structure

Planning Phase

  • Buy one or more buildings if resources allow.
  • Set taxes and ration policy.
  • Assign labor to farms, timber, repairs, road works, militia training, or construction.
  • Post rewards and contracts on map locations or categories.
  • Recruit available characters from unlocked buildings.

Resolution Phase

  • Characters choose tasks based on reward, need, personality, distance, danger, and party fit.
  • Movement and fog reveal resolve along paths.
  • Encounters happen where parties and hostiles overlap or where a site triggers an event.
  • Production, upkeep, trade, taxes, wounds, deaths, and morale update.

Resolution should be readable. The game may simulate many actors, but the player needs a concise turn report: discoveries, gains, losses, threats, shortages, and decisions required next turn.

7. Economy Model

The economy should be deeper than Majesty, but still ruler-facing. The player makes policy and investment decisions instead of moving individual workers.

Resource Source Use Pressure
Coin Taxes, trade, ruin tribute, market fees, crown grants Wages, bounties, buildings, equipment subsidies, diplomacy Running out stops construction and makes characters leave
Food Castle fields worked by farmers, hunting, trade, fishing villages Population upkeep, character supplies, winter reserve Shortage reduces morale, growth, and recruitment
Timber Forests, sawpit, trade Early buildings, road works, tools, palisades Overcutting increases distance and native tension
Stone Hills, quarry, trade Castle upgrades, bridges, walls, shrine work Heavy transport depends on roads and security

8. Castle Buildings

Buildings are purchased from a fixed castle screen. They unlock economy functions, character classes, policies, and map interactions. No exact placement is chosen.

The player controls the castle-settlement only. It starts without farm fields; early food stability comes from farmer activity, hunting, and later trade rather than free farmland placed at map generation.

Building Unlocks Strategic Role
Great Hall Tax policy, edicts, basic contracts, council reports Administrative heart; upgrades increase command reach and contract slots
Market Merchants, equipment availability, trade caravans Turns road safety and village relations into coin and supply
Barracks Guards, patrol contracts, militia, road escorts, landless knights Stabilizes known land and protects farms, bridges, and caravans
Hunter's Lodge Hunters, scouts, forest contracts, game supply Early exploration and food support in woods and meadows
Adventurers' Inn Freelancers, party formation, rumor board Attracts self-directed characters who respond strongly to bounties
Smithy Weapon/armor tiers, repair economy, iron demand Converts materials into survival and combat capability
Chapel Priests, morale rites, burial, limited blessings Low-magic support against fear, curses, undead, and despair
Scribe Tower Map archives, better scouting reports, ruin research Improves fog memory, rumor quality, and old-site interpretation
Roadwarden Office Road projects, bridge repairs, patrol zones, tolls Makes the prototype's roads a full economic and security system

9. Autonomous Characters

Characters are hireable or attracted by the castle's buildings. They belong to classes, have needs, gear, risk tolerance, loyalty, personal purse, wounds, traits, and map knowledge. They are not directly ordered tile by tile.

Scouts

Reveal fog quickly, avoid fights, value exploration rewards, bring rumors about terrain, ruins, and native tracks.

Hunters

Use forests and meadows for food income, track beasts, discover dens, and provide early ranged combat.

Guards

Prefer patrols, escorts, and defense contracts. Keep roads, farms, bridges, and villages functional.

Farmers

Claim up to four viable plots near the castle and cycle between those fields to produce food for the settlement.

Landless Knights

Ambitious mounted fighters without estates. They seek pay, glory, ransom, grants, and dangerous contracts that may eventually earn them land or office.

Adventurers

Seek ruins and dungeons when rewards justify danger. Spend loot on market gear and tavern recovery.

Priests

Support injured parties, counter curses, improve morale, and make certain supernatural sites approachable.

Envoys

Visit towns, villages, and native settlements to negotiate access, trade, warnings, tribute, or peace.

AI rule of thumb: characters score possible actions by reward, distance, danger, personal need, known information, class preference, group availability, and confidence. The player should be able to infer why a character chose a task.

Current Goal System

The prototype should move from per-step opportunistic scoring to a lightweight goal system. Each actor keeps one explicit current goal at a time, such as exploring a promising frontier tile, checking a ruin, establishing a castle farm, harvesting an existing field, hunting a forest patch, patrolling a road segment, returning to the castle for supply, or resting after injury. This makes behavior more legible and avoids scouts changing direction every step because a neighboring tile scores slightly better.

Goal Field Purpose Example
Type Names the intent driving the actor. Explore, Hunt, Farm, Patrol, Delve, Resupply, Rest, Flee
Target Stores the tile, site, road segment, or home building the actor is trying to reach. Unknown edge at 42,18; castle stores; ruin at 77,31
Path Stores the planned route to the target, recalculated only when invalid or stale. List of map coordinates from current tile to target
Commitment Prevents excessive goal switching while still allowing interruptions. Minimum 2 turns unless wounded, blocked, or a better urgent goal appears
Utility Records why this goal was chosen and allows debugging/report text. Explore score 84: unknown edge, nearby ruin rumor, low danger

Goal selection should happen at actor decision points, not every movement step. Typical triggers are: no current goal, target reached, path blocked, actor low on supplies, actor wounded, danger exceeds confidence, goal becomes stale, or the player posts a higher-value contract. The actor then evaluates candidate goals, chooses one, computes a path, and follows that path across turns.

The first implementation should keep the pathfinder simple and deterministic. Use the existing terrain travel costs and an A* search over the virtual map. Road, bridge, and farm tiles should be attractive for travel; deep water and peaks remain impassable; swamps, deep woods, mountains, and unknown tiles carry danger or uncertainty penalties. For unknown frontier exploration, the target should usually be a known tile adjacent to unknown space, not an unseen tile itself, so actors plausibly walk toward the frontier they can reason about.

Implementation approach: add actor.goal with { type, target, path, utility, committedUntilTurn }; generate candidate goals from contracts, visible ruins, frontier edges, roads, forests, castle farm plots, actor needs, and castle safety; rank candidates by class-specific utility; compute an A* path to the selected target; move along the stored path during the animated turn; resolve site actions when the target is reached; then decide whether to keep, complete, or replace the goal.

10. World Map Systems

System Design Direction
Fog of War Unknown tiles are hidden. Characters reveal tiles by sight radius while travelling. Forest, hills, mountains, night events, and weather can reduce visibility.
Travel Cost Keep prototype costs as a foundation: roads and bridges are fastest, plains easy, forests/hills slower, deep woods/swamps punishing, peaks and deep water generally impassable.
Roads Roads connect settlements and become strategic assets. They improve trade, recruitment, patrol speed, tax collection, evacuation, and information flow.
Ruins and Dungeons Old sites contain coin, relics, materials, curses, monster nests, prisoners, documents, and map fragments. They should be tempting but uncertain.
Towns and Villages Non-player settlements buy and sell goods, offer rumors, hire services, request protection, resist taxes, or become strategic partners.
Castle Farms The castle-settlement starts without fields. Farmer actors claim up to four nearby viable plots and then visit those fields to produce food.
Native Settlements Rare local communities with their own claims, taboos, routes, and knowledge. They are not simple enemies; diplomacy and exploitation both have consequences.
Hostiles Monsters, bandits, raiders, undead, and rival claimants can navigate the world map, raid farms, ambush roads, occupy ruins, or migrate away from pressure.
Seasons Use a restrained two-season model for the initial design: summer and winter. Summer supports farming, road work, scouting, and campaigning. Winter stresses food reserves, slows travel, reduces income, and makes shelter, roads, and stores matter without requiring a full four-season terrain palette.

11. Threat Model

Threats should create a living frontier instead of static combat icons. The next prototype step should replace implicit terrain danger with explicit enemy actors that occupy map cells, move during turn resolution, and can fight autonomous characters. Hostiles have simple goals, needs, fear, territory, aggression, and movement rules.

Early Threats

Boars, wolves, bears, small bandit groups, goblin bands, poachers, road ambushes, farm raids, and lost travelers.

Midgame Threats

Organized bandits, cursed ruins, hostile spirits, marauding warbands, mine infestations, native retaliation, and rival noble agents.

Threats should pressure the economy: a monster that blocks a bridge, a bandit camp that makes tolls impossible, or a cursed ruin that scares hunters can be more interesting than a direct attack on the castle.

Enemy Actor Types

Enemy Map Role Behavior Early Counterplay
Boars Low-level forest and meadow danger. Wander locally, attack when disturbed, can wound scouts and hunters but rarely pursue far. Hunters, small bounties, avoiding dense woods while wounded.
Wolves Mobile pack threat around forests, roads, and winter food pressure. Track weak or isolated actors, harass farms, retreat from strong guards. Patrols, hunters, travelling in pairs, winter food reserves.
Bears Rare heavy beast near deep woods, hills, and lairs. Mostly territorial, very dangerous in direct fights, not interested in roads unless provoked. Postpone, hire stronger fighters, gather multiple actors, mark as danger.
Bandits Human economic threat on roads, bridges, farms, and trade routes. Prefer ambush, theft, tolls, and retreat. Become bolder if roads are unpatrolled. Guards, patrol contracts, bounties, road safety investment.
Goblins Folkloric frontier raiders tied to ruins, caves, and old woods. Move in small bands, raid at edges of settled land, may occupy ruins or steal supplies. Scouting, guards, landless knights, clearing lairs before they grow.

Hostile Actor Model

Hostiles should use the same broad map rules as friendly characters: position, glyph, hit points, movement budget, current goal, known territory, and simple pathfinding. They do not need full inventories or complex personalities in the MVP. Their behavior should be legible enough that the player can understand why a road became unsafe or why a hunter was attacked.

Field Purpose Prototype Example
Type Defines stats, glyph, preferred terrain, aggression, and rewards. Wolf, Boar, Bear, Bandit, Goblin
Goal Stores the current intent, using the same idea as friendly actor goals. Wander woods, stalk prey, raid farm, return to lair, flee
Territory Keeps movement local and prevents every enemy from crossing the whole map. Origin tile plus radius, lair tile, road segment, forest patch
Aggression Controls whether the hostile attacks, stalks, avoids, or flees. Boar defends, wolf stalks weak targets, bandit avoids guards
Threat Value Feeds UI warnings, bounty value, and automatic combat odds. Boar 1, wolf pack 2, bear 4, bandits 2-3, goblins 2

Encounter and Combat Resolution

Combat remains automatic. An encounter triggers when a friendly actor and hostile actor end movement on the same tile, cross into each other's immediate engagement range, or when a site action reveals a hostile occupant. The turn animation should show movement first, then the report should summarize only major outcomes: wounds, deaths, routed enemies, cleared lairs, stolen supplies, or valuable discoveries.

  • Odds: compare friendly combat value, hostile threat value, current HP, terrain, supplies, gear, class advantage, and whether either side is surprised.
  • Outcomes: avoid, flee, wound, kill, rout, loot, or capture. Low-level deaths remain possible, especially for scouts caught alone.
  • Terrain: forests favor wolves and hunters, roads favor guards and bandit ambushes, hills and caves favor bears or goblins, castle-adjacent tiles favor defenders.
  • Reports: minor avoided encounters can stay in the console log; wounds, death, ruin fights, lair clearing, or farm raids should open the in-game report.
  • Economy hooks: enemies can reduce road safety, steal food or coin, scare hunters away from a forest, block a bridge, or create bounty opportunities.

Initial implementation approach: add a small game.hostiles array with boar, wolf, bear, bandit, and goblin templates; spawn a few known or hidden hostiles near suitable terrain outside the initial castle reveal; render visible hostiles on the world grid; give them simple current goals and terrain-cost movement; resolve automatic combat when friendly and hostile actors meet; remove the old random danger check once explicit hostile encounters cover wounds and deaths.

12. Setting and Magic Tone

The game is medieval frontier fantasy with restrained magic. Magic is not an everyday artillery system. It appears through saints' relics, old stones, curses, hedge charms, strange weather, ruins, and rare practitioners.

  • Magic should solve specific problems at a cost, not replace economy and logistics.
  • Most characters should be mundane: hunters, guards, landless knights, masons, merchants, envoys, priests, scouts.
  • Supernatural sites should feel old and local rather than cosmic.
  • The crown, church, guilds, and native traditions may interpret magic differently, creating political tension.

13. Suggested MVP

The next playable milestone should prove the relationship between castle decisions, autonomous characters, fog, and map rewards.

Milestone Scope Success Criteria
MVP 1: Explorable Frontier Fog of war, 4 character classes, turn button, autonomous movement, castle farm setup, ruin discovery, explicit early hostile actors, and automatic combat. The player can fund scouting, see the map open over several turns, and understand concrete enemy threats on the frontier.
MVP 2: Castle Economy Castle view, 6 buildings, coin/food/timber/stone, upkeep, taxes, construction queue. Building choices change what characters can do and what pressures appear.
MVP 3: Living Roads Road safety, patrol contracts, trade, bandits and beasts, farms and villages affected by hostile movement. The player cares about defending known land, not only discovering new land.
MVP 4: Diplomacy and Hostile Migration Native settlements, envoys, reputation, moving hostile groups, lairs. The world reacts to expansion and exploitation in legible ways.

Implementation progress, May 28, 2026: MVP 1 is now in progress in the single-file prototype.

  • Done: mobile-first fixed character grid, in-grid resource/status bars, in-grid city mockup, dedicated in-grid actor details screen, fog of war data, initial castle reveal, four autonomous character classes, farmer-created castle fields, in-grid next-turn control, actor current goals with A* pathfinding, terrain-cost movement budgets with visible movement animation before reporting, simple city-selected contracts that steer autonomous behavior, ruin discovery rewards, explicit hostile actors, visible enemy glyphs, automatic combat with flash feedback, and a persistent in-grid turn report view.
  • Still rough: goals are still generated from broad candidate categories, hostile goals are simple territory movement, farming has no seasonality or worker risk model yet, contract effects are broad global priorities rather than location-specific bounties, and report text is functional rather than fully designed.
  • Next target: add location-specific bounties or map pins so player intent can target concrete tiles, threats, and road segments.

14. Decisions and Open Questions

Decided for now

  • Combat resolves automatically. Important encounters should produce readable summaries and logs rather than opening a tactical battle view.
  • Low-level character death can be common. The marches are dangerous, and early hires should feel vulnerable until infrastructure, equipment, experience, and party behavior improve.
  • Seasons are part of the design, initially as summer and winter only, keeping map presentation readable and avoiding too many terrain color variants.
  • Population is represented as one settlement value for now, with labor allocation and morale handled at the settlement level rather than as individual households or classes.

Still open

  • Should the crown be mostly a scenario timer, a mission giver, or a political actor with taxes and demands?
  • Are native settlements fixed cultural factions, procedurally generated communities, or a mix?
